Highlights

Just 28% of survey respondents reported having had sex before — a stark contrast the Centers for Disease Control national data which shows that more than half of U.S. teens have had sex by age 18. Recruited athletes were more than twice as likely as non-recruits to have been intimate, the ‘Prince’ survey found, with respondents who are men, have significant others, or use Tinder on a regular basis also coming in above the overall average. Religious individuals reported less sexual experience than their secular peers.
Just over half of respondents reported experience consuming a full alcoholic beverage in a non-religious context. The median age at surveyed individuals first did so was 11th grade, though over 10% had already imbibed by the time they entered high school. Recruited athletes and those who graduated from boarding schools were more likely to have drank earlier than their general peers, as were those who had had sex; 80% of those who have had sex reported having downed an alcoholic beverage in a non-religious context.
New Jersey’s recent legalization of recreational marijuana likely came as exciting news to the roughly 25 percent of respondents who said they had tried marijuana recreationally. Just 11.7 percent had smoked cigarettes or another tobacco product, but 18.8% had used a vape or electronic smoking device. In this respect, the incoming class are not so different from their peers: numbers reported to the ‘Prince’ in line with the national rate of electronic smoking device use among high school students.
Fewer than two percent of survey respondents have tried hard drugs like cocaine and amphetamines. 4.4 percent have tried psychedelics —, double the rate from last year’s survey. Another substance the Class of 2025 seems to avoid is caffeine; 56.3% of respondents said they don’t drink coffee weekly.
Instagram is the most popular social media network among the Class of 2025, with 91.9% of respondents engaging with it on a regular basis. Snapchat is next, followed by Tik Tok. Just a little over a quarter of respondents use Twitter, Facebook, and Reddit. Consequently, respondents spend quite a bit of time on their phone, clocking in with a daily median of 5-6 hours.
The Class of 2025 listens to a mix of genres, from K-Pop to Country. Pop reigns supreme on the playlists of survey respondents, followed by Alternative/Indie and Rap. Showtunes, Latin, and Metal are viewed less favorably, receiving the attention of 12.4, 12.1, and 5.1 percent of respondents respectively.
The trifecta of sex, drugs, and rock n’ roll is less common than pop culture would make it seem. In reality, only 33.2% of those who listen to rock music regularly have had sex, and 32.5% have tried marijuana recreationally, engaging with the two at rates only nominally higher than their peers. Hip hop listeners are slightly more likely to be participating in these activities, at 37.3% and 34.2%, respectively. In contrast, those who listen to classical music and showtunes are less likely to have had sex or tried marijuana.
Sometimes school got in the way of certain other extracurriculars. Respondents who took more than six APs in high school were less likely to report having had had sex. Similarly, those who reported studying more than 20 hours a week were less likely to have had sex than their peers, with only 18.7% of those with intensive study habits reporting intimacy.
More than a quarter of respondents have received mental health counseling or therapy, nearly double the rate at which the general U.S. adult population attains similar services, and more than half have considered seeking out counseling.
Nearly half of respondents anticipate joining an eating club on campus, but non-legacies tended to be more uncertain. Those receiving financial aid were also less certain; just 41.7 percent of students receiving aid yes, as opposed to 63.4% of those paying full tuition. Far fewer students were interested in joining Greek Life on campus, with only 3.9% saying they would. Nearly 20% reported not knowing the University has fraternities and sororities.
